Significance

Why the claim matters.

P versus NP is the divide between efficient verification and efficient discovery. This release claims that the divide can be crossed by deciding SAT in polynomial time through a locked NAND residual-slack construction and checker-accepted package.

If independently verified, the claim would support an affirmative resolution of P versus NP and would require careful reassessment of optimisation, automated reasoning, program verification, design automation, and assumptions built on computational hardness.

If independently verified

What it would mean for everyone else.

For a general reader, the core idea is simple: many problems are easy to check once someone gives you an answer, but appear extremely hard to solve from scratch. P versus NP asks whether that gap is real.

A foundational question would change status

If the claim passed independent review, it would mean one of the central open questions in mathematics and computer science had an affirmative answer: efficiently checkable problems can also be solved efficiently in the P versus NP setting.

Search, optimisation, and automation would be reassessed

Scheduling, routing, circuit design, automated reasoning, verification, planning, and many scientific search problems are shaped by the belief that some searches are inherently difficult. A verified result would force that belief to be rebuilt from first principles.

Security assumptions would need careful review

Some cryptographic systems rely on problems being hard to solve, not merely hard to check. A verified constructive route would not instantly break every deployed system, but it would trigger urgent review of which assumptions still hold in practice.

Important nuance

True would not mean instantly practical.

An affirmative P versus NP result would be enormous even before it became a usable tool. Real-world impact would depend on the algorithmic details, constants, implementations, and follow-up work needed to turn the theory into practical methods.
